# 01 — National Rules (Trafikförordningen)

These rules apply everywhere in Sweden, regardless of which municipality
you are in. They are not displayed on signs in most cases — they are
expected knowledge. Local rules (Lokala TrafikFöreskrifter, LTF) modify
or override these on specific streets via signage.

## Legal substrate

- **Trafikförordningen (1998:1276)** — TrF, the traffic ordinance. The
  primary federal-level rulebook.
- **Lag (1976:206) om felparkeringsavgift** — the law authorizing
  parking fines on municipal land.
- **Lag (1984:318) om kontrollavgift vid olovlig parkering** — the law
  authorizing private-land parking control fees.
- **Förordningen (2007:231) om elektroniskt kungörande av vissa
  trafikföreskrifter** — requires LTFs to be published electronically
  in STFS to be enforceable.

## Definition of "parking"

A vehicle is considered parked whenever it is stationary, **except**
when stopped for one of these reasons:
- Avoiding danger
- Being required by traffic conditions
- Loading/unloading
- Dropping off or picking up passengers

Stopping to use a phone, even briefly, counts as parking.

## "Stopping is forbidden" implies "parking is forbidden"

Anywhere stopping is prohibited, parking is also prohibited. The reverse
is not true.

## The 10-metre rule (most-cited fine cause for tourists)

It is forbidden to **stop or park** within 10 metres before:
- A pedestrian crossing
- A bicycle crossing or bicycle passage
- An intersecting bicycle path or footpath
- A junction (10m before AND after)

Stopping/parking is permitted **immediately after** a pedestrian
crossing — the 10m rule applies only on the approach side.

This rule is **not signed**. It is simply expected. This is the trap
that catches foreign drivers most often.

Source: Polisen (Swedish Police Authority); Transportstyrelsen.

## The 24-hour rule

A vehicle may not be parked at the same spot on a public road for more
than 24 hours on weekdays (Mon–Fri). The clock restarts on the next
working day, so a vehicle parked Friday 13:00 must be moved by Monday
midnight at the latest. Weekends do not count toward the 24 hours.

Enforcement: parking attendants chalk wheel positions or use ANPR. If
the wheels are in the same position 24h later, a fine is issued. The
common workaround is to move the vehicle even one metre forward or back
each day to reset the clock — note this is a defensive practice, not a
documented loophole.

## Stopping/parking absolute prohibitions (no signs needed)

It is always forbidden to stop or park:
- Where a "No stopping or parking" sign is displayed
- On a solid yellow line
- Where it creates a traffic hazard or unnecessary obstruction
- At a junction or within 10 metres
- Where you obscure visibility (hilltops, blind bends) or signs
- In an underpass or tunnel
- On a road with a solid centre line where the gap to the line is
  under 3 metres
- On a roundabout
- In a public-transport lane
- In a bicycle lane
- In a prohibited zone
- At rail/tram crossings (within 30 metres)
- On a motorway or motortrafikled (clearway)
- On off-road public places (e.g. grass) within urban areas
- Within 10m before crossings (see above)

## Parking absolute prohibitions

In addition to all stopping prohibitions, parking is forbidden:
- Where a "No parking" sign is displayed
- On a broken yellow line
- Where the vehicle is facing against the direction of traffic
  (wrong side). Exception: not applicable if there is a parking sign.
- On a priority road. Exception: not applicable if there is a parking sign.
- Blocking someone's exit
- Alongside another vehicle (double parking).
  Exception: parking next to a two-wheeler is allowed.

## Direction of parking

Vehicles may only be parked on the **right-hand** side of the road, in
the direction of traffic. Exceptions: one-way streets, or roads with a
railway/tramway track on the right.

## Bus stops and tram stops

No parking 20 metres before, or 5 metres after, a bus or tram stop.
Stopping to drop off/pick up is permitted only if it does not disrupt
public transport.

## Disabled parking

Parking spaces marked for disabled drivers (sign T7) require a valid
permit. Without a permit, only stopping for drop-off/pick-up is allowed.
The permit must be **visible** to a parking attendant — proving you have
a permit after the fact is not a valid defence.

## Lighting at night

When parking on the side of a road in darkness, parking and tail lights
must be on so the vehicle is visible to other road users.

## Other always-on rules

- Keys must not be left in a parked vehicle.
- Parking on pedestrian areas, cycle paths, gågator (pedestrian streets)
  is never permitted regardless of the errand.

## Who decides what (authority hierarchy)

| Authority | Scope |
|-----------|-------|
| Trafikverket | Speed limits 80–120 km/h, weight-class rules on national roads, conditions on bridge/road class 4 |
| Länsstyrelsen (county board) | LTFs for roads outside urban areas where the state is roadkeeper; lower speed limits; dangerous-goods rules; race events |
| Kommunen (municipality) | LTFs within urban areas, all parking rules within urban areas, defining what counts as "tättbebyggt område" (urban area) |
| Polismyndigheten | Emergency LTFs when waiting for the municipality is not feasible |
| Trafikkontoret (Stockholm) | Enforcement, signage, parking-meter operation in Stockholm |

Source: Transportstyrelsen — Trafikföreskrifter overview.

## Why this hierarchy matters for a parking-rules platform

When evaluating "is parking allowed here right now," the rule stack is:
1. National prohibitions (Trafikförordningen) — apply everywhere
2. Local LTFs (registered in STFS, exposed in Stockholm via LTF-Tolken)
3. Signage exceptions (the LTF says one thing, the sign on this exact
   street narrows or extends it)
4. Private-land terms (Kontrollavgift contract — applies on tomtmark
   only; entirely outside the LTF system)

Always evaluate top-to-bottom. A national prohibition cannot be
overridden by a local sign permitting parking, but a local LTF can
narrow a national permission.
